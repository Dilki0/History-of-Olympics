The Olympic Games are a thrilling international sports event held every four years, where athletes from over 200 countries come together to compete in various sports like swimming, gymnastics, track and field, etc. Originating in ancient Greece, the modern Olympics promote friendship and cooperation among nations while showcasing incredible athletic talent. Athletes strive for gold, silver, and bronze medals, making the Olympics a celebration of dedication, competition, and cultural diversity.

Time for New Shifts

The ancient Olympic Games were banned in 393 CE by Roman Emperor Theodosius I because he wanted to promote Christianity and stop pagan festivals, which included the Olympics. Since the games were closely linked to Greek religious practices, they were seen as incompatible with the new Christian beliefs. As a result, the Olympic came to an end and sooner forgotten for many centuries.

It wasn’t until the late 1800s that people became interested in reviving the Olympics, leading to the first modern Olympic Games in Athens in 1896, near 1,500 years after the ancient games were banned.

The revival of the Olympic Games in the modern era was primarily driven by Baron Pierre de Coubertin, a French educator and historian. He believed that international sporting events could promote peace and understanding between nations. Inspired by the ancient Olympic Games in Greece, he wanted to create a similar event for the modern world where athletes from different countries could come together and compete in a sprint of friendship.

In 1894, Coubertin founded the International Olympic Committee (IOC) to oversee the organization of the modern Olympic Games, mainly responsible for planning and managing the event. Marking the first modern game, the inaugural Olympic Games were held in Athens, Greece, in 1896, paying homage to the ancient Games’ birthplace. Athletes from 13 countries competed in events like track and field, gymnastics, and swimming, beginning the tradition of international sports competition that continues today.

Major Milestones:

significant historical moments
involving politics

The first modern Olympic Games in 1896 had fewer than 250 athletes, all of whom were men. Over time, the Olympics grew, with more countries joining and new sports being added to the program.

In 1900, female athletes were allowed to compete for the first time, starting with sports like tennis and gold. Today, the Olympics feature thousands of athletes from around the world, competing in a wide variety of sports, including many for both men and women.

1936 Berlin

The Olympics were held in Nazi Germany, and Adolf Hitler tries to use the Games to show off his ideas of racial superiority. However, American athlete Jesse Owens, an African-American, won four gold medals, proving that talent and skill know no race.

1968 Mexico City

During the medal ceremony, two American Athletes, Tommie Smith and John Carlos, raised their fists in a Black Power salute to protest racial inequality. This moment became one of the most famous acts of protest in sports history.

1972 Munich

A tragic event occurred when a terrorist group attacked the Olympic Village took Israeli athletes hostages, resulting in the death of 11 people. This event shocked the world and changed security at the Olympics forever.

1980 Moscow & 1984 Los Angeles

These Olympic were affected by the Cold War, a time of tension between the U.S. and the Soviet Union. In 1980, the U.S. and many countries boycotted the Moscow Games, and in 1984, the Soviet Union and its allies boycotted the Los Angles Games. This made both Games smaller and more political.

THE WINTER OLYMPICS

Alpine skiing was first included in the Olympic Games in 1936 at the Winter Olympics held in Garmisch-Partenkirchen, Germany.

Curling was first introduced in 1924, yet it wasn't official up until 1998 at the Nagano Games in Japan.

The Winter Olympics were first held in 1924, created to showcase sports that take place on snow and ice. Unlike the Summer Olympics, which feature events like running and swimming, the Winter Games focus on sports like skiing, ice, skating, and ice kockey.

Snowboarding was introduced to the Olympic Games in 1998 at the Winter Olympics in Nagano, Japan.

These Games gave athletes a chance to compete in cold-weather sports, and they quickly became just as popular as the Summer Olympics. Today, both the Summer and Winter Olympics are held every four years, but in different seasons.

Bobsleigh was first introduced in 1924 at the Winter Olympics in Chamonix, France.
